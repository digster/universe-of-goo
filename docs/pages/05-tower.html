05 · Tower stability

Why does a World-of-Goo-style tower stand at all? Because every rectangle is secretly two triangles. Without the diagonal, the quadrilateral is a floppy hinge; with it, the whole cell is rigid.

The rigidity trick

A quadrilateral with only its four edges constrained has one degree of freedom: it can shear into a rhombus without any edge changing length. Adding one diagonal forces the two triangles formed to be rigid, because a triangle with all three side lengths fixed cannot deform without changing one of them — basic Euclidean geometry.

The tower in the demo is built level-by-level: two columns of particles per level, horizontal crossbars, vertical columns, and optionally two diagonals per cell. Toggle triangulate off and the tower shears over; on, and it stands rigid under its own weight.

Reading the stress colour

Each constraint is drawn with a hue based on its stressRatio = currentLength / restLength: 

t   = clamp(|ratio - 1| / 0.5, 0, 1)
hue = 120 · (1 - t)       // green at rest, red under load

Green means "near rest length"; red means the constraint is stretched or compressed. Watch which diagonals light up when the tower leans — that's where the load is flowing.

Live demo

Toggle triangulation to compare. Pinned particles at the base are drawn gold; free particles are blue. With triangulation on, only small stresses appear under gravity. Without, the whole stack collapses in seconds.

The real game adds one more layer on top: strand maxStretch. Without it, any tower stands forever — no matter how cruel your level design is.
