03 · Chain / rope

String N particles together with single-stick constraints and you have a rope. Pin one end, drag the other, crank maxStretch down — the chain snaps at its weakest link.

One stick repeated

The chain has no new physics: it's 20 particles and 19 sticks. What makes it interesting is that the relaxation loop from the last page now has to propagate corrections along the whole chain. That's why World.step() runs its constraint pass multiple times per frame — a single pass only carries information between immediate neighbours.

Breaking on overstretch

Every constraint has a maxStretch multiplier. When a stick's current length exceeds restLength · maxStretch, the solver flips c.broken = true and skips correction. After the step, World prunes broken constraints from its array — we don't delete during iteration because that'd skip elements or invalidate indices. 

if len > restLength · maxStretch:
    c.broken = true
    return         // leave endpoints where they are — rope is cut

// ... at end of step():
world.constraints = world.constraints.filter(c => !c.broken)

Live demo

Drag the tail — watch the pinned head stay put while tension propagates up the rope. Slide max stretch down and pull harder until the chain snaps. Reset to reform it.

Why iterate?

With only one pass per frame, a pull at the tail would take 19 frames to reach the pinned head. That's noticeable lag at 60 fps. Eight passes already feels rigid; twenty is near-indistinguishable from a true stiff rope. The cost is linear in iterations × constraints, which is why the game caps at 8 and the sandbox lets you retune live with +/−.
