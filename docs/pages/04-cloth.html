04 · Cloth grid

A rectangular lattice of particles with neighbour-constraints behaves like a sheet of cloth. The trick is adding shear diagonals — without them, the grid collapses flat at the first sideways nudge.

Structural vs shear constraints

Each cell of the grid has four corners. Connect them edge-to-edge (up/down/left/right) and you get structural constraints — these keep the fabric from stretching in either principal axis. But a square can still fold to a rhombus without changing any of those four lengths.

Add a shear diagonal (or both diagonals) and any rhombus-deformation lengthens one diagonal while shortening the other — which the constraint solver resists. Together they approximate the fabric's resistance to in-plane deformation. 

for each cell (x, y) in grid:
    structural:  (x, y) ─ (x+1, y)
                 (x, y) ─ (x, y+1)
    shear:       (x, y) ─ (x+1, y+1)     # diagonals weaken stretching
                 (x+1, y) ─ (x, y+1)     #   to rhombus shapes

Bending constraints (skipping every other particle) can be added for stiffer cloth — omitted here to keep the demo focused.

Pinning corners

The top row has several particles pinned (invMass = 0). The constraint solver gives pinned endpoints zero weight, so the surrounding fabric falls under gravity while the tacks hold firm.

Live demo

Toggle shear off to watch the cloth deflate sideways. Drag any particle with the mouse to sculpt the sheet; the relaxation loop handles the rest.

Toggling shear rebuilds the world — it doesn't add/remove constraints live. That keeps the demo code focused on the observation (shear matters) rather than constraint-array bookkeeping.
